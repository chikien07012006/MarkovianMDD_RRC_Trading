Section 01

The Problem

When a clinician or automated system observes a patient's symptoms, the task is to retrieve a ranked list of plausible diagnoses from a large disease space. This is not straightforward classification: the number of candidate diseases exceeds 17,000 in modern biomedical databases, symptom presentations are incomplete and overlapping, and the reasoning chain from symptom to diagnosis often requires traversing multiple biological concepts — phenotypes, genes, pathways — before reaching a specific conclusion. PrimeKG

A patient presenting with fever, joint pain, and rash does not map to a single disease prototype. The same triad appears in rheumatic fever, lupus, Lyme disease, and adult-onset Still's disease, among others. The fundamental challenge is that a query with K semantically distinct signals should generate K distinct retrieval hypotheses — not collapse them into a single blurred centroid.

Core Gap

Existing systems either use a single dense vector (losing multi-hypothesis capacity) or rely on rigid rule-based traversal (failing on novel symptom combinations). No system simultaneously encodes multiple clinical hypotheses per query, grounds them in a structured biomedical knowledge graph, and retrieves diagnoses via multi-hop reasoning across biological scales.

Why Multi-Interest Encoding

The MIND architecture (Multi-Interest Network with Dynamic Routing), originally proposed for large-scale e-commerce retrieval at Tmall, demonstrated that representing one user as K distinct capsule vectors — each learned by dynamic routing over interaction history — substantially outperforms single-vector models. MIND '19 Industrial deployment showed multi-interest representations improve HitRate and Recall on billion-scale item sets. MedMIG-CR asks whether the same architecture can provide analogous gains on a 17,000-disease, 4-million-edge biomedical knowledge graph — with each capsule serving as a beam-search anchor into the KG rather than a nearest-neighbor query over an item catalogue.

The key architectural novelty is the application of capsule routing not to user behavior sequences, but to symptom co-occurrence patterns across clinical interaction data. Each resulting interest vector should encode a distinct clinical hypothesis cluster (infectious, rheumatologic, metabolic, etc.) that drives a separate beam of KG traversal.

Section 02

PrimeKG — The Reasoning Substrate

PrimeKG (Precision Medicine Knowledge Graph) is a multimodal knowledge graph for precision medicine published in Scientific Data (2023) by Chandak, Huang, and Zitnik at Harvard Medical School. PrimeKG '23 It integrates 20 high-quality biomedical resources to describe 17,080 diseases across 4,050,249 relationships spanning ten major biological scales. These scales span disease-associated protein perturbations, biological processes and pathways, anatomical and phenotypic scales, and the complete range of approved and experimental drugs — including indication, contraindication, and off-label-use edges that are absent from most competing graphs.

Two Structural Challenges

Hub nodes. High-connectivity nodes — well-studied genes like TP53, generic anatomical terms, central metabolic pathways — act as hubs with tens of thousands of one-hop neighbors. Naive two-hop expansion from any symptom node can reach billions of candidate edges, LogosKG overwhelming retrieval systems that do not penalize hub inflation. Research in biomedical KG embedding confirms that models are systematically biased toward high-degree nodes regardless of biological signal. NDAS '24

Embedding-space fragmentation. PrimeKG node embeddings encode graph-structural position (via TransE, SVD, or GNN aggregation), while clinical sequence encoders produce embeddings in a language-model representation space. These two spaces are not aligned by default. Every cosine similarity computed during beam search depends entirely on how well the query encoder's output maps into KG embedding space — making alignment the highest-leverage architectural intervention.

Why PrimeKG Is the Right Graph

For symptom-to-disease retrieval, the critical properties are: coverage of phenotype–disease relationships bridging observable symptoms to diagnoses, multi-scale connectivity enabling multi-hop reasoning from molecular to clinical levels, and a scale large enough to stress-test retrieval systems. PrimeKG satisfies all three, and it is the exact substrate used by both COMIC and LogosKG — the two most relevant 2025–2026 competitors.

Section 04

Three Research Questions

The three RQs form a sequentially dependent chain. RQ3 (evaluation harness) must be built first — it defines the measurement platform for all empirical claims. RQ1 (alignment) is the highest-leverage architectural fix. RQ2 (path reranker) sits on top, requiring aligned interest vectors as input. Together they constitute the minimum viable paper.

RQ3 · Priority 1 — Evaluation Harness
How does multi-interest (K=3) retrieval compare to single-vector (K=1) retrieval and to LogosKG on standard clinical benchmarks?
"Does a K-capsule query encoder improve Recall@{5,10,20,50} and MRR on DDXPlus and ProbSum over PrimeKG, and does the performance gap grow with query symptom heterogeneity?"
The Gap

The MedMIG-CR repository contains no evaluation script. No Recall@K, MRR, or NDCG is computed anywhere. LogosKG (Apr 2026) uses DDXPlus (~1.3M synthetic patients) DDXPlus '22 and ProbSum on PrimeKG as its exact evaluation setup — making these the natural comparison targets. LogosKG

  • Does K=3 outperform K=1 on Recall@20 and MRR, and does the gap grow monotonically with the number of symptoms per query (stratified by DDXPlus disease category)?
  • Does symptom-overlap hard-negative sampling (in interactions.py:44–53) produce harder negatives than random, and does negative difficulty correlate with retrieval performance? COMIC found degree-based negatives outperform random by 10.2%. COMIC
  • Does depth-stratified reporting (Recall@K at 2-hop, 3-hop, 4-hop) reveal distinct performance profiles for shallow vs. deep paths?

Implementation scope: Wrap the existing beam search in an evaluation loop over interactions_test.csv. Estimated engineering effort: <1 week. This is the prerequisite gating all other RQs.

RQ1 · Priority 2 — Cross-Space Alignment
Can a contrastive InfoNCE objective ground each K interest capsule in PrimeKG embedding space — without collapsing them to a single attractor?
"Does InfoNCE alignment between each capsule and its most-relevant PrimeKG disease node outperform fixed-projection beam search, and does a diversity regularizer prevent capsule collapse?"
The Gap

The placeholder linear projection in retrieval_engine.py:38–40 maps interest vectors into PrimeKG embedding space with no learned parameters and no training signal. BALI (2025) demonstrates that an InfoNCE alignment loss between LM entity representations and UMLS KG subgraph embeddings (via a 5-layer GAT) yields 13.1–24.2% zero-shot entity linking gains. BALI '25 BALI aligns a single text representation per concept; MedMIG-CR must align K capsule vectors per query — a novel extension.

Per-capsule InfoNCE alignment loss:

Eq. 1 — Per-capsule alignment objective
ℒ_align = -(1/K) Σ_k log [ Σ_{d ∈ D⁺} exp( cos(v_k, g_d) / τ ) ──────────────────────────────────────── Σ_{d' ∈ D} exp( cos(v_k, g_d') / τ ) ] where v_k = k-th interest capsule vector g_d = PrimeKG node embedding of disease d τ = temperature (learnable or fixed) D⁺ = ground-truth disease set for query
  • Does InfoNCE (Eq. 1) outperform single-anchor BCE alignment? BALI ablations confirm InfoNCE beats classification-based alignment because temperature normalization distributes gradient signal rather than collapsing to one attractor. BALI '25
  • Does the alignment objective cause capsule collapse (avg. pairwise cosine similarity among K vectors → 1.0), and does the diversity regularizer in mind_medmigcr_model.py:46–55 prevent it?
  • What is the optimal weighting λ in the combined loss ℒ_total = ℒ_BCE + λ · ℒ_align?
RQ2 · Priority 3 — Path Coherence Encoding
Does a GRU over relation-type sequences, applied post-hoc as a path reranker, outperform the stateless additive per-hop score?
"Can a 1-layer GRU over PrimeKG relation-type sequences replace the additive cumulative score as a path reranker, within 2× beam search latency overhead — and does the gain concentrate on paths of length ≥3 hops?"
The Gap

MedMIG-CR's current per-hop score (score += α·cos_sim − β·log(degree+1)) is stateless: it assigns no meaning to the sequence of relations traversed. A 4-hop chain of clinically specific relations and a 4-hop chain of molecular noise relations receive the same score if per-hop cosine similarities cancel. COMIC's path masking branch yields +14.82% over single-step approaches COMIC; GRAPE (ICLR 2026) shows lightweight relation-sequence encoders match LLM-based reasoning at 85% lower latency. GRAPE

GRU Reranker Architecture:

Architecture — Post-hoc path reranker
Input: (r₁, r₂, …, r_L) — PrimeKG relation-type IDs (30 types) Encoder: 1-layer GRU, hidden dim = 64 input = learned relation-type embedding table (30 × 32) Output: h_L → linear head → scalar re-score Training: BPR pairwise ranking loss paths → ground-truth diseases (positive) paths → non-associated diseases (negative) Constraint: beam search unchanged; reranker adds O(K × L) cost post-hoc (K=50 paths × L=5 hops = 250 GRU steps)
  • Does relation-type-aware reranking improve Recall@20 over the additive baseline, and by how much relative to COMIC's +14.82% path-masking gain? COMIC
  • Does the gain concentrate on long paths (≥3 hops)? PrimeKG has a long-tail hop distribution extending to 4–5 hops; LogosKG an additive scorer over 4 hops accumulates compounding noise where a GRU encoding the chain as a sequence does not.
  • Does the reranker's learned scoring function act as a proxy for clinical interpretability — preferring paths with higher fractions of disease_phenotype, drug_indication, and phenotype_phenotype edges over purely molecular edges?

Latency fallback: If the GRU exceeds 2× overhead, replace with a linear model over relation-type frequency histograms — slower than additive but faster than GRU, providing graceful degradation.

Section 05

Dependency Map & Minimum Viable Paper

Sequential dependency — build in this order
RQ3
Evaluation Harness (build first) Wraps beam search in a loop over interactions_test.csv. Computes Recall@{5,10,20,50} and MRR on DDXPlus + ProbSum + PrimeKG. Establishes K=1 vs. K=3 baseline. All other RQs are blocked until this exists.
RQ1
Cross-Space Alignment (highest architectural impact) Replaces the untrained placeholder projection with a learned InfoNCE-aligned linear layer. Provides aligned interest vectors as input to beam search. Without this, hub penalty and path scoring improvements are built on an ungrounded encoder.
RQ2
Path Coherence Reranker (highest novelty) GRU over relation-type sequences applied post-hoc on top of the aligned retrieval system. Independent ablation point in the shared evaluation harness. Connects to clinical explainability (future work).

The minimum viable paper combines all three contributions: (1) first evaluation of multi-interest KG retrieval on DDXPlus + ProbSum + PrimeKG with K=1 vs. K=3 ablation; (2) InfoNCE-trained projection replacing the placeholder, with collapse diagnostic; (3) GRU path reranker as post-hoc reranking on top-K beam paths. Each contribution has a clean, independent ablation point, and together they answer: does a properly aligned, path-coherent multi-interest retrieval system outperform the heuristic baseline and compare favorably to LogosKG?
